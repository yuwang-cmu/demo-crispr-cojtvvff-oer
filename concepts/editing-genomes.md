# Concept Map — Editing Genomes

> **Demonstration only:** this AI-generated Coursewerk material is not intended for direct classroom or clinical use.

## Source and scope

This chapter adapts the version-pinned Wikipedia articles **CRISPR gene editing**, **Genome editing**, **DNA repair**, and **Cas9**. It explains design logic and evidence without providing operational wet-lab instructions, therapeutic recommendations, or claims that one delivery or repair strategy is universally superior.

## Summary

Genome editing links a programmable targeting system to cellular chemistry. Delivery determines where, when, and how long an editor is present. Nuclease-based editors can generate DNA breaks that are processed by end joining, microhomology-mediated pathways, or homology-directed repair. Base and prime editing change the design space but still produce context-dependent outcome distributions and possible by-products. Validation must measure the intended locus, unintended changes, allele frequencies, molecular function, and phenotype with appropriate controls.

## Objectives

1. Separate target design, delivery, molecular action, repair, selection, and validation in an editing workflow.
2. Predict why end joining and template-supported repair yield different but overlapping outcome distributions.
3. Compare DNA, RNA, and ribonucleoprotein delivery as trade-off bundles rather than rankings.
4. Distinguish on-target efficiency, on-target heterogeneity, off-target activity, functional effect, and toxicity.
5. Design a layered validation strategy that supports a bounded claim.
6. Compare ethical and governance questions across somatic, germline, agricultural, and ecological contexts.

## Concept sequence

1. A question and intended change define the claim to be tested.
2. Target, editor, and delivery choices determine molecular exposure.
3. Editing chemistry can create a break, a base conversion, or a templated rewrite.
4. Cellular processing turns the initial event into a distribution of alleles.
5. Target-locus assays, controls, and orthogonal methods characterize molecular outcomes.
6. Transcript, protein, function, and phenotype measurements support progressively broader claims.
7. Ethics and governance shape the initial question, intervention context, and acceptable interpretation.

## Misconceptions to surface

- “Precise targeting” does not imply one precise repair outcome.
- A high fraction of altered sequencing reads does not by itself establish functional rescue.
- Off-target analysis does not replace checking unexpected on-target rearrangements or mixed alleles.
- Base and prime editors avoid a programmed double-strand break but are not consequence-free.
- Heritable editing raises issues not resolved by technical accuracy alone.
